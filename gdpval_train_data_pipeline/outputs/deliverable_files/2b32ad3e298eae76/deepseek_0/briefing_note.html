Comparative Financial Stability Vulnerability Assessment – November 2025

Euro Area & United States — Briefing Note for Bilateral Policy Meeting

1. Executive Summary

This note compares financial stability vulnerabilities in the euro area (EA) and the United States (US), drawing exclusively on the ECB Financial Stability Review (May and November 2025) and the Federal Reserve Financial Stability Report (November 2025). Both jurisdictions face an elevated and broadly synchronized risk environment, dominated by trade policy uncertainty, stretched asset valuations, and rising non-bank financial intermediation (NBFI) leverage. However, the nature and location of vulnerabilities differ materially.

The US exhibits more acutely stretched asset valuations — equity price-to-earnings ratios near the upper end of their historical range, corporate bond spreads at exceptionally tight levels — alongside a notable build-up of hedge fund and life insurer leverage. The euro area confronts a more macro-financially entangled risk set: corporate vulnerabilities are elevated in tariff-sensitive manufacturing, while sovereign debt sustainability concerns are resurfacing amid higher defence spending and rising interest costs. Cross-border transmission — through trade, capital flows, and the dollar activities of European banks — could amplify a shock originating in either jurisdiction into a systemic event. Both reviews identify policy uncertainty, geopolitical risks, and a sudden repricing of risk premia as the most salient near-term triggers. Overall risk rating: US — Elevated; EA — Elevated.

3. Common Risks and Notable Divergences

3.1 Most Salient Common Risks

  1. Trade policy uncertainty as the dominant macro-financial shock. Both the ECB and Fed identify trade policy — specifically US tariff actions and associated uncertainty — as the pre-eminent risk. US effective tariff rates have risen sharply; global trade policy uncertainty remains well above historical averages. The Fed's Survey of Salient Risks ranks "policy uncertainty" as the most frequently cited risk. The April 2025 tariff announcements triggered a sharp sell-off across both jurisdictions not seen since early COVID-19, before markets partially recovered after a 90-day tariff pause.
  2. Stretched asset valuations vulnerable to sudden repricing. Both jurisdictions exhibit limited valuation buffers. The US shows more extreme readings — forward P/E near historical peaks, equity risk premia near 20-year lows, corporate spreads substantially below medians. The EA faces the compounding effect of high risk concentration and NBFI liquidity fragilities that could amplify repricing.
  3. NBFI leverage and liquidity mismatches. Both reports flag rising non-bank leverage. The Fed highlights hedge fund leverage across Treasury, rates, and equity strategies, plus life insurer leverage in the top historical quartile. The ECB documents increasing financial and synthetic leverage among EA investment funds and deepening bank–NBFI interconnectedness. In both cases, these could transmit stress via fire-sale dynamics and counterparty exposures.
  4. Geopolitical risks and geoeconomic fragmentation. Both reviews identify geopolitical tensions — including the risk of broader geoeconomic fragmentation — as key tail risks. The ECB FSR (May 2025) notes that uncertainty across trade, regulatory, fiscal, and international cooperation domains raises the likelihood of "increasingly frequent and impactful adverse tail events."

3.2 Notable Divergences

  1. Sovereign debt sustainability: a distinctly European vulnerability. The EA faces sovereign debt challenges with no direct US parallel. Higher issuance and funding costs could strain weak sovereigns; fiscal slippage may trigger renewed fragmentation (ECB FSR Nov. 2025, Chart 3). Defence spending needs — with the national escape clause activated for 11 EA countries — compound these pressures. Several EA countries have debt exceeding 100% of GDP with fiscal balances near or above the 3% Maastricht threshold.
  2. Corporate vulnerability: sectoral concentration vs. aggregate resilience. EA corporate vulnerabilities are acute in tariff-sensitive manufacturing, with elevated bankruptcies and declining order books. US business and household debt/GDP is at 20-year lows, though consumer credit delinquencies (credit cards, auto loans) remain above pre-pandemic levels. The EA's high trade openness makes its corporate sector disproportionately exposed to trade disruptions.
  3. US-specific NBFI leverage patterns. The Fed provides granular detail on hedge fund and life insurer leverage with no equally prominent counterpart in the ECB assessment. The ECB's concern centres on investment fund leverage and bank–NBFI interconnectedness.
  4. Diverging CRE dynamics. US CRE prices are declining (nominal −5.6% y/y), while EA real estate markets were described in May 2025 as "recovering" though facing headwinds from elevated uncertainty.

4. Cross-Border Transmission Channels

  1. Trade and supply-chain contagion. Tariff escalation directly hits the EA's open, export-oriented economy. Tariff-sensitive sectors account for significant shares of EA employment, gross value added, and credit. The ECB identifies "rolling recessions" — where manufacturing shocks cascade into services and consumption — as a systemic threat (Box 1, ECB FSR Nov. 2025).
  2. US Treasury market dysfunction → global spillovers. The April 2025 episode showed that Treasury market liquidity strains generate global repercussions. The atypical sell-off in US Treasuries — partly from unwinding of basis trades and margin calls — raised questions about the safe-haven status of US assets. The ECB notes investors may be "reassessing the riskiness of US assets, possibly leading to broader shifts in global capital flows" with "far-reaching consequences" (ECB FSR May 2025).
  3. Capital flow reversals. The rotation of fund flows from the US to the EA observed in early 2025 could reverse abruptly, exposing EA non-banks to redemption pressures and forced asset sales.
  4. US dollar activities of European banks. The ECB FSR (Nov. 2025, Box 3) examines European banks' dollar activities, highlighting potential vulnerabilities if US money market conditions tighten or dollar funding becomes strained.
  5. Sovereign–bank nexus amplification. Within the EA, renewed sovereign fragmentation could damage bank balance sheets via sovereign bond holdings, raising funding costs and impairing lending. While primarily intra-EA, this could be triggered by external shocks including US policy actions.

5. Forward-Looking Assessment: Possible Triggering Shocks

Given the identified vulnerabilities, the following shocks could plausibly trigger a financial stability event over the next 6–12 months:

  1. Trade policy escalation. A breakdown of trade negotiations, substantially higher tariffs, or a full trade war remains the most immediate risk. Both the ECB and Fed identify this scenario as having potential for "significant adverse impacts on global growth, inflation and asset prices" (ECB FSR May 2025). Trade policy uncertainty is already affecting firm financing conditions in Europe.
  2. Sudden repricing of risk premia. With US equity valuations stretched and corporate spreads historically tight, even a moderate macro reassessment could trigger sharp repricing. The Fed survey identifies "a sharp decline in asset prices, potentially connected to a turn in the cycle" as a key concern. Given global market interconnectedness, such repricing would not be contained within the US.
  3. Geopolitical shock. Intensification of conflicts — whether related to defence, cyber threats, or geoeconomic fragmentation — could simultaneously hit growth, raise risk aversion, and disrupt commodity markets, with asymmetric effects on the energy-importing euro area.
  4. Sovereign stress in the euro area. Reassessment of sovereign credit risk, triggered by higher defence spending, fiscal slippage, or political uncertainty, could reignite the sovereign–bank nexus. Several high-debt EA countries face substantial near-term refinancing needs.
  5. Disorderly NBFI deleveraging. Rising hedge fund and investment fund leverage creates conditions for fire-sale dynamics, where margin calls or redemptions force rapid asset sales, depressing prices and transmitting stress to banks via counterparty exposures. The April 2025 episode provided a preview.

Policy Focus Recommendations

  1. For both jurisdictions: Close monitoring of NBFI leverage and liquidity risk is essential. The April 2025 episode underscored how margin calls and redemption pressures can disrupt even the deepest markets. Regulatory work to address data gaps and enhance NBFI resilience should be accelerated.
  2. For the euro area: Macroprudential authorities should maintain or build releasable capital buffers given elevated corporate vulnerabilities and sovereign–bank feedback loop risks. The deepening bank–NBFI linkages warrant enhanced surveillance. Fiscal–financial stability coordination is critical: defence spending must be met without triggering sovereign stress that damages financial intermediation.
  3. For the United States: Given stretched valuations and rising leverage, supervisory attention to hedge fund exposures — particularly in Treasury markets — and life insurer leverage should remain high. The recovery of Treasury market liquidity from April lows is welcome but does not eliminate structural intermediation vulnerabilities.
  4. Cross-border coordination: The interconnectedness of these risks — through trade, capital flows, and the global role of the dollar — demands close transatlantic dialogue. Joint contingency planning for tail events involving Treasury market dysfunction or EA sovereign stress would enhance preparedness.
