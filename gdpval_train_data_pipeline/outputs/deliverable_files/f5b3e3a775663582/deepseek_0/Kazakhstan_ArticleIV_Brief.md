# Kazakhstan: Article IV–Style Country Brief — Macroeconomic Stability and Debt Sustainability

**Prepared by:** IMF Country Desk for Kazakhstan  
**Date:** July 2025  
**Data Source:** World Bank Kazakhstan Economic Update, Winter 2024–2025, Table 1 (WBG staff estimates and projections)  
**Coverage:** 2020–2026 (2024–2026 are forecasts)  
**Fiscal Definition:** Consolidated budget (central government, local governments, and the National Fund of the Republic of Kazakhstan, NFRK). Government debt includes state and local government debt and government guarantees; excludes SOE debt.

---

## 1. Macroeconomic Overview

### 1.1 Real GDP Growth: Resilient Non-Oil Engine, Volatile Oil Sector

Kazakhstan’s economy has demonstrated considerable resilience since the COVID-19 contraction of 2020. After a sharp recession in 2020 (real GDP −2.5%), growth rebounded to 4.3% in 2021 and remained robust at 3.2% in 2022 despite the Russia-Ukraine shock and double-digit inflation. Growth accelerated further to 5.1% in 2023, driven by a surge in oil output (+8.3%). The outlook projects sustained momentum of 4.0% in 2024, 4.7% in 2025, and a moderation to 3.5% in 2026.

A key structural feature is the divergence between the oil and non-oil sectors:

| Year | Real GDP Growth (%) | Oil Sector (%) | Non-Oil Sector (%) |
|------|--------------------:|---------------:|-------------------:|
| 2020 | −2.5 | −5.8 | −1.3 |
| 2021 | 4.3 | −0.4 | 5.2 |
| 2022 | 3.2 | −0.8 | 3.9 |
| 2023 | 5.1 | 8.3 | 4.0 |
| 2024f | 4.0 | 0.4 | 4.6 |
| 2025f | 4.7 | 8.2 | 3.9 |
| 2026f | 3.5 | 1.3 | 3.9 |

Non-oil growth has been relatively stable, averaging approximately 4.2% over 2021–2026, which suggests a broadening of the productive base. However, aggregate GDP remains susceptible to oil-sector volatility: the 2025 forecast embeds an 8.2% oil-sector expansion, presumably linked to the Tengiz expansion (Future Growth Project). A delay or underperformance would materially reduce headline growth.

**Turning point:** The economy transitioned from pandemic-induced contraction (2020) to above-trend growth (2023), but the projected deceleration to 3.5% by 2026—coupled with declining oil prices—signals a return to the lower post-2014 trend growth rate.

### 1.2 Inflation: Persistent and Above Target

CPI inflation (end-of-period) remains the most significant near-term macroeconomic vulnerability:

| Year | CPI Inflation, eop (%) | Policy Rate, year-end (%) |
|------|-----------------------:|--------------------------:|
| 2020 | 7.5 | 9.00 |
| 2021 | 8.5 | 9.75 |
| 2022 | 20.3 | 16.75 |
| 2023 | 9.8 | 16.50 |
| 2024f | 8.4 | 16.25 |

Inflation spiked to 20.3% at end-2022—a multi-decade high—driven by global food and energy price pass-through, tenge depreciation, and strong domestic demand. The National Bank of Kazakhstan (NBK) responded with aggressive tightening, raising the policy rate from 9.75% at end-2021 to 16.75% by end-2022. Disinflation has been gradual: end-2023 inflation stood at 9.8%, and the forecast for end-2024 is 8.4%, still far above the NBK's 5% target. Only by 2026 is inflation projected to approach 6.0%, implying a prolonged period of restrictive monetary policy.

**Vulnerability:** The slow pace of disinflation—despite rates held at 16.25–16.75% for two years—suggests entrenched inflation expectations and strong indexation mechanisms. Real policy rates, while positive, may not be sufficiently restrictive if inflation expectations remain unanchored above 8%.

### 1.3 Fiscal Balances: Narrowing Revenue Base, Persistent Deficits

The consolidated fiscal position evolved as follows:

| Year | Revenues (% GDP) | Expenditures (% GDP) | Balance (% GDP) |
|------|------------------:|---------------------:|----------------:|
| 2020 | 16.1 | 24.5 | −8.4 |
| 2021 | 16.8 | 21.9 | −5.1 |
| 2022 | 21.5 | 21.6 | −0.2 |
| 2023 | 21.5 | 23.1 | −1.6 |
| 2024f | 19.7 | 22.3 | −2.5 |
| 2025f | 19.7 | 22.9 | −3.1 |
| 2026f | 19.6 | 22.2 | −2.7 |

After a large pandemic-driven deficit (−8.4% of GDP) in 2020, fiscal consolidation and the commodity price windfall brought the budget to near balance (−0.2%) in 2022. However, the structural deterioration is evident in the outer years: revenues decline from 21.5% of GDP (2022–2023) to 19.6–19.7% (2024–2026) as oil prices fall from their 2022 peak ($99.8/bbl Brent) to $73.0/bbl by 2026. Expenditures remain elevated at 22.2–22.9% of GDP, yielding deficits of 2.5–3.1% of GDP.

**Key concern:** The revenue base is narrow and pro-cyclical—oil exports generate roughly one-third of tax collections—and the projected expenditure path shows no meaningful consolidation. The implied structural primary deficit at current oil prices ($73–81/bbl) is estimated at around 1.3–1.7% of GDP.

### 1.4 External Sector: Deteriorating Current Account, Shrinking Buffers

The current account (CA) balance has been highly volatile:

| Year | CA (% GDP) | Exports (% GDP) | Imports (% GDP) | Net FDI (% GDP) | Brent ($/bbl) |
|------|-----------:|----------------:|----------------:|----------------:|--------------:|
| 2020 | −6.4 | 28.8 | 27.2 | 4.2 | 42.3 |
| 2021 | −1.4 | 36.4 | 25.2 | 2.3 | 70.4 |
| 2022 | +3.1 | 41.5 | 26.7 | 2.2 | 99.8 |
| 2023 | −3.3 | 34.3 | 27.4 | 2.0 | 82.6 |
| 2024f | −1.3 | 32.9 | 26.2 | 0.8 | 81.0 |
| 2025f | −2.2 | 31.2 | 25.5 | 1.4 | 75.0 |
| 2026f | −2.3 | 29.1 | 25.0 | 1.7 | 73.0 |

The 2022 surplus (+3.1% of GDP) was entirely oil-price driven and has reversed as Brent declined from $100 to $73–81/bbl. The CA is projected to remain in deficit (−1.3% to −2.3% of GDP) throughout the forecast horizon. Net FDI has weakened substantially—from 4.2% of GDP in 2020 to 0.8% in 2024—implying that CA deficits are increasingly financed by portfolio flows or reserve drawdowns rather than stable equity-like inflows. The recovery in net FDI to 1.4–1.7% of GDP by 2025–2026 is welcome but remains well below historical norms.

### 1.5 Government Debt: Low but on a Gradually Rising Path

Government debt is low by international standards:

| Year | Gov. Debt (% GDP) | Domestic (% GDP) | External (% GDP) | YoY Change (pp) |
|------|------------------:|-----------------:|-----------------:|----------------:|
| 2020 | 24.9 | 14.1 | 10.8 | — |
| 2021 | 23.7 | 13.8 | 9.9 | −1.2 |
| 2022 | 22.5 | 13.9 | 8.6 | −1.2 |
| 2023 | 22.0 | 15.1 | 6.9 | −0.5 |
| 2024f | 22.2 | 15.1 | 7.1 | +0.2 |
| 2025f | 23.1 | 16.5 | 6.6 | +0.9 |
| 2026f | 23.9 | 17.7 | 6.2 | +0.8 |

The debt ratio declined from 24.9% of GDP in 2020 to a trough of 22.0% in 2023, benefiting from high nominal GDP growth (the denominator effect) and fiscal consolidation. However, the trajectory has turned: debt is projected to rise to 23.9% by 2026. The composition is shifting toward domestic debt (from 14.1% to 17.7% of GDP), which reduces currency risk but may crowd out private credit if absorbed by the domestic banking system.

## 2. Fiscal and External Sustainability Indicators

### 2.1 Government Debt Service Burden

| Year | Debt Service (% of Revenues) | Debt Service (% of GDP) | Implicit Debt Service Rate¹ (%) |
|------|----------------------------:|------------------------:|-------------------------------:|
| 2020 | 6.8 | 1.09 | 4.4 |
| 2021 | 7.3 | 1.23 | 5.2 |
| 2022 | 6.5 | 1.40 | 6.2 |
| 2023 | 7.4 | 1.59 | 7.2 |
| 2024f | 8.2 | 1.62 | 7.3 |
| 2025f | 7.3 | 1.44 | 6.2 |
| 2026f | 7.3 | 1.43 | 6.0 |

> ¹ *Implicit debt service rate = (Debt Service / GDP) ÷ (Government Debt / GDP). This overstates the pure interest rate as debt service includes principal amortization; it is used here as a rough upper bound.*

**Interpretation:** Government debt service absorbed 6.5–8.2% of revenues over the period—a moderate burden by international standards (the IMF typically flags emerging-market vulnerabilities above 15–18%). However, the rise from 6.5% in 2022 to 8.2% in 2024 is notable: it reflects the combined effect of higher domestic interest rates and the gradual repricing of the domestic debt stock. At 1.6% of GDP, the absolute debt service burden remains manageable but the upward trajectory warrants monitoring, particularly if the NBK maintains elevated policy rates for longer than projected.

### 2.2 Public Debt-to-GDP Path: From Decline to Gradual Rise

The year-on-year change in the debt ratio can be decomposed into contributions from the snowball effect (interest-growth differential) and the primary balance:

| Year | Δ Debt/GDP (pp) | Snowball Contribution² (pp) | Primary Balance Proxy³ (% GDP) | SFA Residual (pp) |
|------|----------------:|----------------------------:|-------------------------------:|------------------:|
| 2021 | −1.2 | −1.7 | −3.9 | −3.4 |
| 2022 | −1.2 | −3.3 | +1.2 | +3.3 |
| 2023 | −0.5 | −1.5 | −0.0 | +1.0 |
| 2024f | +0.2 | −1.0 | −0.9 | +0.3 |
| 2025f | +0.9 | −1.3 | −1.7 | +0.5 |
| 2026f | +0.8 | −0.7 | −1.3 | +0.3 |

> ² *Snowball = (r − g) / (1 + g) × d₋₁, where r is the implicit debt service rate (nominal) and g is the nominal GDP growth proxy (real GDP growth + CPI inflation).*  
> ³ *Primary balance proxy = consolidated budget balance + debt service as % of GDP (treating all debt service as interest, hence a conservative estimate; the true primary balance is somewhat stronger).*

**Interpretation:** The decline in debt during 2021–2023 was powerfully assisted by the snowball effect: high nominal GDP growth (combining real growth of 3.2–5.1% and inflation of 8.5–20.3%) far exceeded the effective interest cost. This benign r − g differential mechanically reduced the debt ratio by 1.5–3.3 pp per year. As inflation moderates toward 6.0% and real growth toward 3.5%, the nominal growth rate declines to approximately 9.5% by 2026, narrowing the favorable r − g gap. The snowball contribution shrinks from −3.3 pp (2022) to −0.7 pp (2026). At the same time, the primary balance shifts from near zero or small surplus to a deficit of 1.3–1.7% of GDP, turning the overall debt dynamics positive (i.e., debt-increasing).

The stock-flow adjustment (SFA) residual—capturing below-the-line items such as exchange rate revaluation of foreign-currency debt, NFRK asset drawdowns, and recognition of contingent liabilities—has been positive in recent years, partially offsetting the favorable snowball. This warrants further scrutiny: it may reflect the use of NFRK assets to finance the budget deficit (a drawdown of gross assets rather than an increase in gross debt), which preserves the headline debt ratio but depletes the sovereign's net asset position.

### 2.3 External Debt-to-GDP and External Debt-to-Exports

| Year | Total External Debt (% GDP) | Exports (% GDP) | Ext. Debt / Exports (ratio) |
|------|----------------------------:|----------------:|----------------------------:|
| 2020 | 95.8 | 28.8 | 3.33 |
| 2021 | 83.3 | 36.4 | 2.29 |
| 2022 | 71.3 | 41.5 | 1.72 |
| 2023 | 62.0 | 34.3 | 1.81 |
| 2024f | 58.5 | 32.9 | 1.78 |
| 2025f | 56.3 | 31.2 | 1.80 |
| 2026f | 54.3 | 29.1 | 1.87 |

**Interpretation:** The total external debt ratio has declined impressively from 95.8% of GDP in 2020 to a projected 54.3% in 2026—a reduction of over 40 percentage points. This reflects both nominal GDP growth and net debt repayment. The external-debt-to-exports ratio has also improved, from 3.33x in 2020 to 1.72–1.87x in 2022–2026.

However, two caveats apply:

- **Composition matters:** Total external debt includes inter-company lending in the oil sector, which is typically self-liquidating through export receipts. The external sovereign debt component (the difference between total government debt and domestic debt) is modest, at 6.2–10.8% of GDP. State-owned enterprise (SOE) external debt is estimated by the World Bank at an additional ~5% of GDP in 2023 (not included in the government debt definition used here).
- **Denominator vulnerability:** The improvement in the ratio is partly driven by a strong exports denominator in 2022 (41.5% of GDP). As exports decline toward 29.1% of GDP by 2026 (tracking lower oil prices), the external-debt-to-exports ratio edges up to 1.87x, indicating a moderate and manageable burden but one that is sensitive to commodity price assumptions.

### 2.4 Reserve Adequacy

| Year | NBK Reserves (% GDP) | Imports (% GDP) | Import Cover (months) | Reserves / Short-Term External Debt⁴ |
|------|---------------------:|----------------:|----------------------:|-------------------------------------:|
| 2020 | 20.8 | 27.2 | 9.2 | — |
| 2021 | 17.4 | 25.2 | 8.3 | — |
| 2022 | 15.6 | 26.7 | 7.0 | — |
| 2023 | 13.7 | 27.4 | 6.0 | — |
| 2024f | 14.2 | 26.2 | 6.5 | — |

> ⁴ *Import cover = NBK Reserves (% GDP) × 12 ÷ Imports (% GDP). No breakdown of short-term external debt is available in Table 1 for the Guidotti ratio.*

**Interpretation:** Import cover has declined from a comfortable 9.2 months in 2020 to 6.0–6.5 months in 2023–2024. This is above the traditional 3-month adequacy threshold and within the IMF's recommended range of 5–7 months for commodity exporters under the ARA (Assessing Reserve Adequacy) metric for flexible exchange-rate regimes. However, the trajectory is clearly downward—NBK reserves fell from 20.8% to 13.7% of GDP between 2020 and 2023—and the modest recovery to 14.2% in 2024 does not reverse the trend. If the CA remains in deficit and net FDI stays weak, reserve loss is likely to continue, potentially testing the lower bound of adequacy in a stress scenario.

It should be noted that Kazakhstan also holds substantial NFRK assets (projected at 18.2% of GDP by 2026), which provide an additional—though not strictly reserve—liquidity buffer. Combined sovereign foreign assets (NBK + NFRK) were 55.1% of GDP in 2020, falling to 34.9% by 2023 and projected at ~32.4% by 2026.

### 2.5 Current Account and Financing Structure

The CA deficit is projected to persist at 1.3–2.3% of GDP, driven by a structural trade deterioration: exports fall from 41.5% of GDP (2022) to 29.1% (2026) while imports remain sticky at 25.0–27.4% of GDP. Oil exports alone decline from 20.8% to 13.7% of GDP—a 7 pp loss—while non-oil exports are essentially flat at 14.9–20.7% of GDP, underscoring the narrow export base.

Financing the CA deficit is increasingly challenging:

| Year | CA Balance (% GDP) | Net FDI (% GDP) | Residual Financing Gap (% GDP) |
|------|--------------------:|----------------:|------------------------------:|
| 2020 | −6.4 | 4.2 | −2.2 |
| 2021 | −1.4 | 2.3 | +0.9 |
| 2022 | +3.1 | 2.2 | +5.3 |
| 2023 | −3.3 | 2.0 | −1.3 |
| 2024f | −1.3 | 0.8 | −0.5 |
| 2025f | −2.2 | 1.4 | −0.8 |
| 2026f | −2.3 | 1.7 | −0.6 |

In 2024, net FDI covers only about 60% of the CA deficit, leaving a residual financing gap that must be met by portfolio flows, other investment, or reserve drawdowns. The erosion of FDI from 4.2% to 0.8% of GDP over five years is a structural concern that may reflect declining attractiveness of the extractive sector, geopolitical uncertainty, and institutional constraints.

## 3. Debt Sustainability Assessment (Qualitative)

### 3.1 Public Debt Sustainability

Kazakhstan's public debt is low and, under the baseline, does not present immediate sustainability concerns. The debt-to-GDP ratio of 22–24% is well below the IMF's 55–60% benchmark for emerging markets and the 70% threshold commonly used in DSA frameworks for commodity exporters. Even with rising deficits, the debt trajectory remains contained.

The key drivers of debt dynamics are:

- **Favorable growth-interest differential (r − g < 0):** Throughout the projection period, the implicit debt service rate (4.4–7.3%) remains below nominal GDP growth (9.5–23.5%), producing a debt-reducing snowball effect. This is the single most important stabilizer of Kazakhstan's public debt.
- **Moderate primary deficits:** The primary balance is projected at −0.9% to −1.7% of GDP, which is easily financed given the low debt stock and favorable r − g.
- **NFRK assets as a backstop:** Gross government debt of 22–24% of GDP understates the strength of the sovereign balance sheet, as the NFRK holds foreign assets equivalent to 18–21% of GDP. On a net basis, the government is a net creditor.

**Shock scenarios (qualitative):**

| Shock | Transmission Channel | Estimated Impact |
|-------|---------------------|------------------|
| Oil price −$20/bbl (to ~$53) | Revenue loss of ~2–3 pp of GDP; GDP growth −1 to −1.5 pp; tenge depreciation | Primary deficit widens to ~3.5–4.5% of GDP; debt/GDP rises to ~27–29% by 2026 |
| Growth shock (−2 pp p.a.) | Lower nominal GDP growth narrows r − g gap; automatic stabilizers widen deficit | Snowball benefit reduced to near zero; debt/GDP rises to ~26–28% by 2026 |
| Combined oil + growth shock | Simultaneous revenue loss, lower growth, and currency depreciation increase external debt burden | Debt/GDP rises toward 30–32%; external debt-to-exports moves above 2.0x |
| Contingent liability crystallization (SOE debt ~5% GDP) | One-off increase in government debt from assumption of SOE obligations | Debt/GDP jumps by ~5 pp to 27–29%; still manageable but raises gross financing needs |

Even under the most adverse combined shock, public debt is unlikely to exceed 32% of GDP—a level that remains sustainable and financeable. The principal risk is not a sovereign solvency crisis but rather a **liquidity squeeze** if market access is disrupted during a commodity downturn and NFRK assets are depleted simultaneously.

### 3.2 External Debt Sustainability

External sustainability metrics have improved markedly but remain sensitive to commodity prices. Total external debt at 54–62% of GDP is moderate, and the declining trend provides confidence. The external-debt-to-exports ratio of ~1.8x is manageable for a commodity exporter. However:

- The **current account is structurally weak** at projected oil prices of $73–81/bbl. The breakeven oil price for the CA (the price at which the CA balances) appears to be in the range of $85–90/bbl, implying that at the baseline assumption of $73–81/bbl, Kazakhstan runs a structural external deficit.
- **FDI coverage of the CA deficit is inadequate.** With net FDI at 0.8–1.7% of GDP versus a CA deficit of 1.3–2.3%, the external position relies on less stable financing sources.
- **Reserve adequacy is declining.** The loss of ~6 pp of GDP in NBK reserves between 2020 and 2023, if continued, would bring import cover below 5 months within 2–3 years.

**External shock scenario:** A sustained oil price decline to $55/bbl would reduce exports by approximately 5–7 pp of GDP, widen the CA deficit to 4–5% of GDP, and push external debt-to-exports above 2.5x—levels that would raise external vulnerability flags at the IMF. Combined with weak FDI, this would accelerate reserve loss and potentially trigger a disorderly exchange-rate adjustment.

## 4. Risks and Policy Recommendations

### 4.1 Summary of Key Risks and Vulnerabilities

1. **Commodity price dependence (High Likelihood, High Impact).** Oil and gas dominate exports (~50% directly, more indirectly), fiscal revenues (~one-third of tax collections), and GDP (~20%). The baseline already assumes a declining oil price path (Brent from $81 to $73/bbl by 2026), but a sharper correction—plausible given global energy transition and OPEC+ dynamics—would simultaneously weaken growth, fiscal revenues, the current account, and reserve coverage.

2. **Inflation persistence and policy trade-offs (High Likelihood, Medium Impact).** Inflation at 8.4% after two years of 16%+ policy rates indicates structural rigidities (administered prices, high pass-through, dollarized expectations). Premature easing would risk a wage-price spiral; prolonged tightness would constrain credit and growth, and raise domestic debt service costs.

3. **Erosion of fiscal and external buffers (Medium Likelihood, High Impact).** NFRK assets have declined from 34.3% to 20.9% of GDP (2020–2024), and NBK reserves from 20.8% to 14.2% of GDP. These buffers are being drawn down to finance persistent twin deficits. A continued depletion trajectory would leave Kazakhstan exposed to the next commodity downturn with substantially reduced shock-absorption capacity.

4. **Weak FDI and structural external financing gap (Medium Likelihood, Medium Impact).** The halving of net FDI from 4.2% to 0.8% of GDP reduces the quality of external financing and increases reliance on portfolio flows, which are inherently more volatile and sensitive to global risk appetite.

5. **Contingent liabilities from SOEs (Low Likelihood, Medium Impact).** While SOE debt (~5% of GDP in 2023) is excluded from the government debt definition, distress at entities such as Samruk-Kazyna could create contingent claims on the sovereign balance sheet.

### 4.2 Policy Recommendations

**Recommendation 1: Accelerate fiscal consolidation and rebuild buffers.** The projected fiscal deficit of 2.5–3.1% of GDP in 2024–2026 should be reduced toward a structural balance consistent with the long-term oil price assumption (~$65–70/bbl). This requires: (i) implementing the new Tax Code to broaden the revenue base—particularly by raising VAT efficiency (currently ~4% of GDP versus a 7% high-income-country benchmark) and reducing tax expenditures; (ii) anchoring the non-oil fiscal deficit to a declining path consistent with intergenerational equity and NFRK sustainability; and (iii) reprioritizing expenditures toward growth-enhancing public investment, which has fallen to below 15% of total spending.

**Recommendation 2: Strengthen the monetary policy framework to durably reduce inflation.** The NBK should maintain a restrictive stance until inflation is firmly on a path toward the 5% target, supported by: (i) enhanced communication of forward guidance to anchor expectations; (ii) continued exchange-rate flexibility to absorb external shocks without depleting reserves; and (iii) development of the domestic capital market to improve monetary policy transmission and reduce dollarization.

**Recommendation 3: Diversify the export base and improve the investment climate to attract non-extractive FDI.** The declining trend in net FDI—particularly outside the oil sector—requires urgent attention. Policy priorities should include: (i) regulatory simplification and contract enforcement reforms; (ii) targeted infrastructure investment to reduce the landlocked economy's high logistics costs; and (iii) leveraging the country's strategic position in critical minerals and renewable energy supply chains to attract "new economy" FDI. A broader export base would simultaneously reduce external vulnerability, raise potential growth, and reduce fiscal dependence on oil.
