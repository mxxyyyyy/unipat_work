# Kazakhstan: Article IV-Style Country Brief — Macroeconomic Stability and Debt Sustainability

**Prepared by:** IMF Country Desk Economist  
**Data Cut-Off:** Table 1, Kazakhstan Economic Update (World Bank, Winter 2024–2025)  
**Classification:** Internal Dissemination  
**Date:** July 2025

---

## Executive Summary

Kazakhstan's economy has demonstrated resilience through the triple shocks of COVID-19 (2020), the commodity-price surge and inflationary spike (2022), and a period of monetary tightening (2022–2024). Real GDP recovered strongly from a 2.5 percent contraction in 2020 to 5.1 percent growth in 2023, supported by robust oil-sector expansion and steady non-oil activity. The consolidated fiscal deficit narrowed dramatically from 8.4 percent of GDP in 2020 to near balance in 2022, though it has since widened to a projected 2.5–3.1 percent of GDP in 2024–2026. Public debt remains exceptionally low by international standards at 22–24 percent of GDP, affording substantial fiscal space. External vulnerabilities are receding, with total external debt declining from 95.8 percent of GDP in 2020 to a projected 54.3 percent by 2026. However, structural vulnerabilities persist: heavy dependence on oil revenues, declining reserve buffers (both NBK reserves and NFRK assets), weakening net FDI inflows, and sticky inflation that remains above target. This brief assesses Kazakhstan's macroeconomic trends, computes key fiscal and external sustainability indicators, provides a qualitative debt sustainability assessment, and concludes with policy recommendations.

## 1. Macroeconomic Overview

### 1.1 Real Sector: Growth Recovery and Composition

Kazakhstan's growth trajectory over 2020–2026 reveals three distinct phases:

- **Pandemic contraction (2020):** Real GDP fell by 2.5 percent, with the oil sector contracting 5.8 percent and the non-oil sector by 1.3 percent.
- **Recovery and normalisation (2021–2023):** Growth rebounded to 4.3 percent in 2021, moderated to 3.2 percent in 2022 (reflecting the Russia-Ukraine shock and supply disruptions), and accelerated to 5.1 percent in 2023 — the strongest reading in the projection window. Oil-sector growth surged to 8.3 percent in 2023, driven by the ramp-up of Tengiz production. Non-oil growth remained stable at 3.9–5.2 percent.
- **Projected moderation (2024–2026):** Growth is forecast at 4.0–4.7 percent in 2024–2025, moderating to 3.5 percent in 2026 as the oil investment cycle matures. Oil-sector growth is projected to spike to 8.2 percent in 2025 before normalising to 1.3 percent in 2026. Non-oil growth is projected to hold steady at 3.9–4.6 percent.

> **Key turning point:** The 2023 growth acceleration (5.1 percent) was oil-led. The projected deceleration to 3.5 percent by 2026 underscores the need for non-oil productivity growth to sustain medium-term dynamism.

### 1.2 Inflation: Persistent Above-Target Pressures

CPI inflation (end-of-period) rose from 7.5 percent in 2020 to a peak of **20.3 percent in 2022**, driven by global food and energy price pass-through, tenge depreciation, and strong domestic demand. The National Bank of Kazakhstan (NBK) raised the policy rate from 9.75 percent at end-2021 to 16.75 percent by end-2022, maintaining it at 16.25–16.50 percent through 2024. Inflation has since subsided to 9.8 percent (2023) and a projected 8.4 percent (2024), but the disinflation path remains gradual. The 2026 projection of 6.0 percent remains above most inflation-targeting peers.

> **Vulnerability:** Sticky services inflation and second-round effects from fiscal expansion could delay convergence to the NBK's target, prolonging the tight monetary stance and dampening credit-sensitive investment.

### 1.3 Fiscal Accounts: From Crisis Deficits to Moderate Structural Shortfalls

The consolidated fiscal balance (comprising central and local governments and the National Fund of the Republic of Kazakhstan, NFRK) swung from a pandemic-driven deficit of **8.4 percent of GDP in 2020** to near balance (−0.2 percent of GDP) in 2022, buoyed by oil revenue windfalls (Brent averaged $99.8/bbl). Since 2023, the fiscal position has deteriorated, with the deficit widening to 1.6 percent (2023), a projected 2.5 percent (2024), and 2.7–3.1 percent in 2025–2026.

The revenue decline from 21.5 percent of GDP (2022–2023) to 19.6–19.7 percent (2024–2026) reflects the projected fall in Brent from $99.8 to $73.0/bbl. Oil exports as a share of total revenue ranged from 70–97 percent over the period, underscoring extreme fiscal dependence on hydrocarbon receipts. Expenditures are projected to remain elevated (22.2–22.9 percent of GDP), reflecting social transfers, wage commitments, and public investment.

> **Turning point:** The 2022 near-balance was a windfall-driven outcome, not a structural improvement. The projected structural deficit of 2.5–3.1 percent in 2024–2026, financed partly by drawdowns of NFRK assets, signals a gradual erosion of fiscal buffers.

### 1.4 External Sector: Narrowing Deficits, Declining Buffers

The current account balance (CAB) has been volatile, reflecting oil-price cycles:

| Indicator (% GDP) | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Current Account Balance | −6.4 | −1.4 | +3.1 | −3.3 | −1.3 | −2.2 | −2.3 |
| Trade Balance (approx.) | +1.6 | +11.2 | +14.8 | +6.9 | +6.7 | +5.7 | +4.1 |
| Net FDI | 4.2 | 2.3 | 2.2 | 2.0 | 0.8 | 1.4 | 1.7 |

The CAB turned positive in 2022 (+3.1 percent of GDP) on surging oil prices but returned to deficit from 2023 (−3.3 percent) as import demand recovered and oil prices moderated. The trade surplus is projected to narrow steadily from 14.8 percent of GDP (2022) to 4.1 percent (2026), compressing the current account.

Net FDI has declined sharply, from 4.2 percent of GDP in 2020 to a projected low of 0.8 percent in 2024, before a modest recovery to 1.7 percent by 2026. This decline partly reflects the completion of major oil-field investment cycles (Tengiz expansion) and, potentially, investor caution amid geopolitical uncertainty.

### 1.5 Government Debt: Low but Creeping Up

Government debt (state and local government plus guarantees, excluding SOE debt) stood at 24.9 percent of GDP in 2020, declined to a trough of 22.0 percent in 2023, and is projected to rise gradually to 23.9 percent by 2026. The debt composition is shifting toward domestic issuance: domestic debt is projected to rise from 14.1 percent of GDP (2020) to 17.7 percent (2026), reflecting government efforts to deepen the domestic bond market and reduce FX exposure.

> **Key observation:** At 22–24 percent of GDP, Kazakhstan's public debt is among the lowest of any emerging market. However, the *direction* of change — rising from 2024 onwards — merits attention, particularly if primary deficits persist.

## 2. Fiscal and External Sustainability Indicators

### 2.1 Government Debt Service Burden

The debt service-to-revenue ratio measures the share of government revenues consumed by interest and amortisation payments on public debt.

| Indicator | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Debt Service (% of Revenues) | 6.8 | 7.3 | 6.5 | 7.4 | 8.2 | 7.3 | 7.3 |
| Debt Service (% of GDP) | 1.09 | 1.23 | 1.40 | 1.59 | 1.62 | 1.44 | 1.43 |

**Interpretation:** The debt service burden is moderate, rising from 6.8 percent of revenues in 2020 to a projected peak of 8.2 percent in 2024 before easing. In GDP terms, debt service is a modest 1.1–1.6 percent. This reflects the low debt stock and the concessional or moderate terms on Kazakhstan's borrowing. The 2024 peak (8.2 percent) corresponds to the combination of lower revenues and higher effective interest rates. The projected decline to 7.3 percent by 2025–2026 reflects assumed interest-rate normalisation. An 8 percent threshold is well within manageable bounds but should be monitored if revenues underperform.

### 2.2 Public Debt-to-GDP Path and Year-on-Year Changes

| Indicator | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Government Debt (% GDP) | 24.9 | 23.7 | 22.5 | 22.0 | 22.2 | 23.1 | 23.9 |
| YoY Change (pp GDP) | — | −1.2 | −1.2 | −0.5 | +0.2 | +0.9 | +0.8 |

**Debt dynamics decomposition:** The decline in 2021–2023 was driven by favourable snowball effects — nominal GDP growth (real growth plus inflation) of 12.8–23.5 percent mechanically reduced the debt ratio by 1.7–3.3 pp annually, more than offsetting primary deficits or near-zero primary balances. The projected increase from 2024 (+0.2 pp), accelerating to +0.8–0.9 pp in 2025–2026, reflects an adverse turn: as nominal GDP growth moderates toward 9.5 percent (2026) and primary deficits persist at 1.3–1.7 percent of GDP, the debt ratio begins to tick upward.

**Calculated effective interest rate (implied by debt service / debt stock):**

| | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Effective Interest Rate (%) | 4.4 | 5.2 | 6.2 | 7.2 | 7.3 | 6.2 | 6.0 |

The effective rate has risen from 4.4 percent (2020) to 7.3 percent (2024), reflecting higher domestic and international borrowing costs. It is projected to moderate to 6.0 percent by 2026.

### 2.3 External Debt-to-GDP and External Debt-to-Exports Ratios

| Indicator | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Total External Debt (% GDP) | 95.8 | 83.3 | 71.3 | 62.0 | 58.5 | 56.3 | 54.3 |
| External Debt / Exports (%) | 332.6 | 228.8 | 171.8 | 180.8 | 177.8 | 180.4 | 186.6 |
| External Debt YoY Change (pp GDP) | — | −12.5 | −12.0 | −9.3 | −3.5 | −2.2 | −2.0 |

**Interpretation:** The external debt-to-GDP ratio has declined dramatically — by 41.5 percentage points from the 2020 peak — reflecting both GDP growth and net repayments. However, at 54.3 percent of GDP by 2026, external indebtedness remains material. The external debt-to-exports ratio, which fell sharply from 333 percent (2020) to 172 percent (2022), has since stabilised in the 178–187 percent range, as the decline in the debt stock has been matched by a compression of export values (exports falling from 41.5 percent of GDP in 2022 to 29.1 percent by 2026). 

> **Key concern:** While the external debt-to-GDP ratio is on a declining path, the external debt-to-exports ratio has plateaued near 180 percent. This implies that Kazakhstan's external debt remains roughly *1.8 times annual export receipts* — a level that, while manageable under baseline oil-price assumptions, could become strained under a sustained commodity-price downturn. It should be noted that a significant portion of external debt is private-sector and FDI-related (including project finance for oil developments), which mitigates sovereign risk but does not eliminate balance-of-payments pressures.

### 2.4 Reserve Adequacy

| Indicator | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| NBK Reserves (% GDP) | 20.8 | 17.4 | 15.6 | 13.7 | 14.2 | n.a. | n.a. |
| NFRK Assets (% GDP) | 34.3 | 28.1 | 24.7 | 22.9 | 20.9 | 19.3 | 18.2 |
| **Total Sovereign Buffers (% GDP)** | **55.1** | **45.5** | **40.3** | **36.6** | **35.1** | **n.a.** | **n.a.** |
| Import Cover — NBK Reserves (months) | 9.2 | 8.3 | 7.0 | 6.0 | 6.5 | n.a. | n.a. |

*Note: Import cover computed as NBK reserves (% GDP) divided by monthly imports (% GDP / 12). NBK reserves projections for 2025–2026 are not available in the source table. NFRK assets are end-of-period values.*

**Interpretation:** NBK reserves have declined from 20.8 percent of GDP (2020) to 14.2 percent (2024). Import cover stood at a comfortable 9.2 months in 2020 but has fallen to 6.0–6.5 months in 2023–2024. The standard adequacy threshold of 3 months is still comfortably met, but the declining trend is notable. When NFRK assets are included, total sovereign buffers amount to 35.1 percent of GDP in 2024 — a still-substantial cushion, but down from 55.1 percent in 2020. The combined drawdown reflects both fiscal transfers from the NFRK to finance budget deficits and NBK foreign-exchange sales to support the tenge.

> **Reserve adequacy assessment:** NBK-only reserves satisfy the IMF's reserve adequacy metric (ARA) for a floating exchange-rate regime. At 6–7 months of import cover, they also meet the traditional rule of thumb (≥3 months). However, the Guidotti ratio (reserves / short-term external debt) cannot be computed from the available data, as the short-term external debt breakdown is not provided in Table 1. The declining trajectory of both NBK and NFRK buffers warrants close monitoring, especially in a context of persistent current account deficits and uncertain FDI flows.

### 2.5 Current Account Balance and Financing Structure

The current account is projected to remain in deficit (−1.3 to −3.3 percent of GDP) over 2023–2026. The trade surplus, while still positive, is narrowing. The key concern is the financing structure:

- **Net FDI** has declined from 4.2 percent of GDP (2020) to 0.8 percent (2024). At this level, FDI covers only about 60–65 percent of the current account deficit, leaving a residual financing gap that must be covered by portfolio flows, drawdown of reserves, or external borrowing.
- By 2025–2026, a projected recovery in net FDI to 1.4–1.7 percent of GDP would still cover only 64–74 percent of the current account deficit.

A CAB of −2.2 to −2.3 percent of GDP that is only partially financed by stable FDI inflows implies reliance on more volatile sources of external financing, increasing vulnerability to shifts in global risk appetite.

## 3. Debt Sustainability Assessment (Qualitative)

### 3.1 Public Debt Sustainability

Kazakhstan's public debt trajectory is assessed as **sustainable with moderate vulnerability** under the baseline. The debt-to-GDP ratio (22–24 percent) is far below the IMF's 55–60 percent emerging-market threshold. However, the qualitative assessment reveals several nuances:

**Drivers of public debt dynamics:**

1. **Primary balance:** Kazakhstan has run primary deficits in most years (with 2022 as the sole exception, when the primary balance reached +1.2 percent of GDP). The projected primary deficit of 0.9–1.7 percent of GDP in 2024–2026 is the key driver of debt accumulation.
2. **Growth-interest differential:** The effective interest rate on government debt (6.0–7.3 percent) has risen above real GDP growth (3.5–5.1 percent) in recent years, generating an adverse r−g differential. However, high inflation-driven nominal GDP growth (9.5–14.9 percent) continues to produce favourable snowball effects (negative mechanical contribution to debt/GDP), partially offsetting the primary deficit.
3. **Stock-flow adjustments:** The source table does not provide explicit stock-flow adjustment (SFA) data, though exchange-rate effects on FX-denominated debt are likely contributors to debt dynamics given the tenge's volatility.

**Debt-stabilising primary balance:**

| | 2024f | 2025f | 2026f |
|---|---|---|---|
| Actual Primary Balance (% GDP) | −0.9 | −1.7 | −1.3 |
| Debt-Stabilising Primary Balance (% GDP) | −1.0 | −1.3 | −0.8 |

In 2024, the actual primary deficit (−0.9 percent of GDP) is slightly smaller than the debt-stabilising level (−1.0 percent), implying marginal debt reduction. However, in 2025, the projected primary deficit (−1.7 percent) exceeds the stabilising level (−1.3 percent), leading to debt accumulation. By 2026, the gap narrows again.

> **Bottom line:** Under the baseline, public debt is on a gently rising path from 2024, driven by persistent primary deficits. The trajectory remains benign due to low initial debt levels, but the direction signals that the current fiscal stance is mildly expansionary relative to a debt-stabilising position.

### 3.2 Plausible Shock Scenarios (Qualitative)

**Scenario 1: Lower Oil Prices (Brent at $55–60/bbl, a ~25 percent decline from the 2026 baseline of $73)**

- **Transmission:** Oil exports decline by roughly 2–3 percentage points of GDP → government revenues fall by 1.5–2.5 percent of GDP (given that oil-related receipts account for approximately one-third of total tax collection and a larger share of NFRK transfers) → the consolidated fiscal deficit widens to 4.5–5.5 percent of GDP.
- **Debt impact:** With a wider primary deficit of 3–4 percent of GDP, the debt-to-GDP ratio could rise by 2–3 percentage points per year, reaching 27–30 percent of GDP by 2026 — still manageable in absolute terms but a significant deterioration from the baseline.
- **External impact:** The current account deficit could widen to 4–5 percent of GDP. NFRK assets would be drawn down more rapidly, and NBK reserves could come under pressure if the tenge depreciates sharply.
- **Mitigants:** The flexible exchange rate would absorb part of the shock. The low initial debt stock provides fiscal space for a gradual adjustment.

**Scenario 2: Growth Shock (Real GDP 1.5–2.0 pp below baseline)**

- **Transmission:** Weaker growth reduces tax revenues, widens the fiscal deficit, and raises the debt-to-GDP ratio through both the primary balance and a less favourable snowball effect (lower nominal GDP growth).
- **Debt impact:** A 1.5 pp reduction in real GDP growth, combined with a 0.5 pp wider primary deficit (from automatic stabilisers), could add 1.5–2.0 pp per year to the debt ratio. The debt-stabilising primary balance would shift from −1.0 percent to approximately +0.5 percent of GDP, requiring fiscal tightening to prevent debt accumulation.
- **External impact:** Import compression would partially offset the weaker export performance, but the current account deficit could still widen modestly.

**Scenario 3: Combined Shock (Oil Price Decline + Tighter Global Financial Conditions)**

- Under a combined scenario, the fiscal deficit widens, external financing costs rise, FDI weakens further, and the tenge faces depreciation pressure. The debt ratio could reach 30–35 percent of GDP by 2026, with reserves declining below 5 months of import cover. While this remains within manageable bounds for an emerging market, it would represent a material erosion of buffers.

### 3.3 External Debt Sustainability

Kazakhstan's external debt dynamics are assessed as **improving but still vulnerable to export shocks**:

- **Favourable factors:** The external debt-to-GDP ratio has declined by 41.5 pp since 2020, driven by robust nominal GDP growth and net debt repayment. Much of the external debt is private-sector and FDI-related (including inter-company loans in the oil sector), which typically have longer maturities and are tied to export-generating projects.
- **Risk factors:** The external debt-to-exports ratio, at ~180 percent, remains elevated. Should exports decline (via lower oil prices or volumes), the debt-to-exports ratio could spike above 200 percent, a level that has historically triggered external stress in commodity-exporting economies. The declining FDI coverage of the current account deficit implies greater reliance on portfolio flows — a potential source of volatility.

> **Qualitative assessment:** External sustainability hinges critically on the oil-price outlook and continued FDI. At baseline, the external position is manageable. A sustained oil-price decline to $50–55/bbl would raise external sustainability concerns, particularly if accompanied by capital flow reversals.

## 4. Risks, Vulnerabilities, and Policy Recommendations

### 4.1 Main Risks and Vulnerabilities

1. **Oil-price dependency remains the dominant macro-fiscal risk.** Oil exports account for 70–97 percent of consolidated government revenues and approximately 45–50 percent of total exports of goods and services. The projected Brent price path ($73–75/bbl in 2025–2026) is subject to significant two-sided uncertainty from global demand conditions, OPEC+ decisions, and the energy transition. A sustained decline to $50–55/bbl would expose fiscal and external vulnerabilities.

2. **Erosion of fiscal and external buffers.** NFRK assets have declined from 34.3 to 18.2 percent of GDP (2020–2026 projected), and NBK reserves from 20.8 to 14.2 percent (2020–2024). While buffers remain adequate, the pace of drawdown — averaging 2–3 pp of GDP per year — is not indefinitely sustainable. Persistent fiscal deficits financed by NFRK withdrawals will eventually deplete the oil fund's stabilisation capacity.

3. **Structural fiscal weakness.** The non-oil fiscal deficit (not directly computable from Table 1, but inferable from the high share of oil-related revenues) remains substantial. The planned new Tax Code — including higher taxes on the extractive and financial sectors and VAT reform — represents a welcome effort to broaden the revenue base, but implementation risks are material.

4. **Sticky inflation and constrained monetary policy.** Despite the NBK's aggressive tightening (policy rate at 16.25 percent), inflation at 8.0–8.4 percent remains well above most emerging-market targets. Prolonged tight monetary conditions may crowd out private investment, particularly in the non-oil sector.

5. **Geopolitical uncertainty and FDI weakness.** Net FDI has fallen sharply to 0.8 percent of GDP in 2024. While the completion of large-scale oil projects partly explains the decline, geopolitical risks associated with the Russia-Ukraine conflict and secondary sanctions concerns may be deterring non-extractive FDI — precisely the kind needed for diversification.

6. **External refinancing risk.** Although total external debt is declining, the external debt-to-exports ratio (~180 percent) remains elevated, and the short-term external debt profile (not detailed in Table 1) could introduce rollover risk in the event of a sudden stop in capital flows.

### 4.2 Policy Recommendations

**Recommendation 1: Accelerate fiscal consolidation and revenue diversification.**

The projected structural primary deficit of 1.3–1.7 percent of GDP in 2025–2026 should be reduced through a combination of revenue mobilisation and expenditure rationalisation. Priority measures include:
- Implementing the new Tax Code, with emphasis on raising the VAT efficiency (currently well below the high-income country average of 7 percent of GDP) and broadening the CIT base.
- Introducing a credible medium-term fiscal anchor (e.g., a non-oil primary balance target consistent with NFRK asset preservation) to discipline the drawdown of oil savings.
- Rationalising current expenditure, particularly subsidies and transfers, while protecting growth-enhancing public investment.

**Recommendation 2: Strengthen reserve adequacy and external resilience.**

With NBK reserves at 6.0–6.5 months of import cover and declining, the authorities should:
- Rebuild reserve buffers during periods of favourable oil prices through rules-based NFRK accumulation.
- Enhance monitoring of short-term external debt and contingent liabilities (including SOE external debt, estimated at ~5 percent of GDP in 2023), ensuring that the consolidated public-sector external balance sheet is well understood.
- Maintain the flexible exchange rate as the first line of defence against external shocks, limiting FX intervention to disorderly market conditions.

**Recommendation 3: Anchor inflation expectations and support non-oil growth.**

The NBK should maintain its tight policy stance until inflation is firmly on a path toward the target range. Complementary structural policies should target:
- Improving the business climate to attract non-extractive FDI, including through regulatory simplification, contract enforcement, and governance reforms.
- Deepening domestic capital markets to reduce reliance on external financing and support local-currency government borrowing (consistent with the rising share of domestic debt, from 56 percent of total government debt in 2020 to a projected 74 percent in 2026).
- Investing in human capital and infrastructure to raise non-oil productivity growth, which has hovered around 3.9–4.6 percent — insufficient to absorb a growing labour force and reduce the growth gap with aspirational peers.
