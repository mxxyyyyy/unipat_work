# Kazakhstan: Article IV-Style Country Brief

## Macroeconomic Stability and Debt Sustainability Assessment

**IMF Country Desk — Kazakhstan | Data: World Bank KEU Winter 2024–2025, Table 1 | Coverage: 2020–2026 (2024–2026 = projections) | Internal Dissemination**

---

## Executive Summary

Kazakhstan's economy has proven resilient through successive shocks — COVID-19 (−2.5% GDP, 2020), the 2022 inflation surge (CPI 20.3%), and aggressive monetary tightening (policy rate from 9.00% to 16.75%). Real GDP recovered to 5.1% in 2023 and is projected at 3.5–4.7% over 2024–2026. The consolidated fiscal deficit narrowed from 8.4% of GDP (2020) to near balance in 2022, before widening to 2.5–3.1% as oil revenues moderate. Gross government debt is low (22.0–24.9% of GDP) and external debt has declined sharply from 95.8% to 54.3% of GDP.

Despite these strengths, structural vulnerabilities are intensifying: net FDI has halved (4.2% → 0.8% of GDP, 2020–2024), NBK reserves have eroded (20.8% → 14.2% of GDP), NFRK assets are steadily drawn down (34.3% → 18.2% of GDP), and the non-oil fiscal deficit persists. Oil — roughly one-third of fiscal revenues and half of exports — remains the dominant risk factor. The baseline is sustainable, but a sharp oil price decline would accelerate buffer depletion.

**Policy priorities:** (i) diversify fiscal revenues through the forthcoming Tax Code; (ii) rebuild NBK reserves and adhere to the NFRK fiscal rule; (iii) catalyze non-oil FDI through SOE privatization and investment climate reform.

## 1. Macroeconomic Overview

### 1.1 Real GDP Growth and Inflation

*Source: Table 1; f = forecast.*

Growth recovered strongly from the 2020 pandemic trough, with non-oil activity consistently expanding at 3.9–5.2% from 2021 onward. The 2023 acceleration to 5.1% reflected surging oil output (+8.3%) as Tengiz ramped up. Growth moderates to 3.5% by 2026, with the oil sector oscillating between near-zero and 8% depending on field expansion cycles and OPEC+ compliance. Without structural reforms, trend growth appears anchored at 3.5–4.0%.

The 2022 inflation spike to 20.3% — driven by tenge depreciation, global price shocks, and post-pandemic demand — triggered one of the region's sharpest tightening cycles. Disinflation has been gradual: 9.8% (2023), 8.4% (2024e), reaching the NBK's 5% target only by 2026. The policy rate at 16.25% implies deeply positive real rates, constraining private investment through the forecast horizon. Premature easing risks re-igniting inflation.

Expenditures, compressed in 2021–2022, rebounded to 22–23% of GDP from 2023. The structural concern is that expenditures remain sticky at 22–23% of GDP while revenues settle at ~19.6%, yielding a persistent consolidated deficit of 2.5–3.1% of GDP. The non-oil fiscal deficit — not directly measurable from Table 1 but approximated by excluding NFRK oil transfers — is significantly larger.

Oil accounts for 46–50% of exports throughout, underscoring deep structural concentration.

## 2. Fiscal and External Sustainability Indicators

### 2.1 Government Debt Service Burden

The burden is manageable: at 7–8% of revenues (~1.1–1.6% of GDP), well below the IMF's 14–18% indicative threshold for emerging markets. The 2024 peak (8.2%) likely reflects maturing pandemic-era domestic debt. **Caveat:** the rising share of domestic debt (57% → 74% of total, see 2.2) means an increasing portion of new issuance is exposed to the 16.25% policy rate, raising marginal rollover costs.

Public debt is exceptionally low. The decline from 24.9% to 22.0% (2020–2023) was driven by high nominal GDP growth (5–23.5% annually) — the "snowball effect" — and fiscal consolidation. A turning point arrives in 2024: debt rises toward 23.9% by 2026 as nominal growth moderates to ~9.5% and primary deficits persist. The composition shift toward domestic debt (from 57% to 74% of total) reduces FX exposure but increases sensitivity to the elevated domestic rate environment.

External SOE debt (~5% of GDP) is a contingent liability not captured in government debt.

At ~2.6 pp annually, the NFRK faces material depletion within 7–8 years absent oil price recovery or fiscal consolidation.

### 2.5 External Financing Structure

The CA+FDI balance turned negative in 2023 (−1.3% of GDP) and remains so through 2026 (−0.6%). Net FDI covers only 35–74% of the CA deficit in 2024–2026, down from near-full coverage earlier. The trade surplus compresses from +14.8% to +4.1% of GDP as oil fades. This structural shift implies growing reliance on NFRK drawdowns and, eventually, portfolio inflows — a less stable financing mix than FDI.

## 3. Debt Sustainability Assessment (Qualitative)

### 3.1 Baseline: Public Debt Dynamics

The standard debt accumulation equation — **Δd = (r − g) / (1 + g) × d₋₁ − pb + sfa** — operates favorably:

- **(r − g) differential:** Nominal GDP growth of 9.5–12.7% (2024–2026, proxied as real growth + CPI) exceeds the effective interest rate on government debt (estimated below 8%), yielding a negative differential of 2–5 pp. This snowball effect alone reduces the debt ratio by 0.5–1.0% of GDP annually.
- **Primary balance (pb):** The consolidated deficit of 2.5–3.1% of GDP acts as a headwind. The primary deficit (consolidated balance + interest, where interest is a fraction of the 1.4–1.6% of GDP debt service) is estimated at ~2.0–2.5% of GDP — partly offset by the favorable snowball.
- **Stock-flow adjustments (sfa):** Not observable in Table 1. Modest FX valuation effects are possible on residual foreign-currency GG debt (6.2% of GDP in 2026).

The net result — debt rising gradually from 22.0% to 23.9% of GDP — poses no near-term sustainability concern. Even under conservative assumptions, debt remains below 30% of GDP, well under the IMF's 55–60% distress threshold.

### 3.2 Shock Scenario Analysis (2026 Baseline)

#### Shock 1: Oil Price Decline (Brent $73 → $50/bbl, −31.5%)

| Channel | Mechanism | Magnitude |
|:--------|:----------|:----------|
| Exports | Oil exports fall from 13.7% → ~9.4% GDP | CA deteriorates from −2.3% → ~−6.6% GDP |
| Fiscal | Oil-related revenues (~⅓ of tax, ~6.5% GDP) decline | Deficit widens from −2.7% → ~−4.8% GDP |
| Buffers | Counter-cyclical NFRK drawdown | NFRK (18.2% GDP) covers ~3–4 years |
| Debt | Rises from 23.9% GDP | Remains below 30% over 3-year horizon |

Even a sharp oil shock does not threaten near-term debt sustainability given low initial debt and NFRK buffers. Sustained low prices, however, would accelerate NFRK depletion and force pro-cyclical consolidation.

#### Shock 2: Growth Slowdown (−2 pp)

Real growth falls to 1.5% (vs. 3.5% baseline). Nominal growth declines from ~9.5% to ~7.5%, narrowing the favorable (r − g) differential. Additional debt accumulation via snowball ≈ 0.5% of GDP/year; revenue shortfall adds ~0.4–0.5% of GDP to the deficit. Combined impact is manageable but directionally concerning.

#### Shock 3: Combined Tail Risk (Oil + Growth)

Simultaneous oil price collapse and global demand weakness — analogous to 2014–2015 or early 2020 — produces a cumulative external financing gap of ~4–6% of GDP. NFRK could absorb this for several years, but buffer depletion accelerates sharply and macroeconomic trade-offs become acute.

## 4. Risks and Policy Recommendations

### 4.1 Principal Risks

1. **Oil price dependence (High probability, High impact).** Oil = ~⅓ fiscal revenues, ~½ exports. A Brent drop to $50/bbl widens the CA deficit by ~4 pp and the fiscal deficit by ~2 pp of GDP. NFRK buffers are finite.

2. **Declining FDI (Medium probability, Medium impact).** Net FDI has halved to 0.8% of GDP, undermining external financing. CA+FDI is now in structural deficit.

3. **Persistent inflation and high real rates (Medium probability, Medium impact).** CPI not projected to reach 5% until 2026. The 16.25% policy rate constrains private investment and raises domestic debt costs.

4. **NFRK buffer depletion (Medium probability, Medium impact).** NFRK assets have fallen from 34.3% to 18.2% of GDP (2020–2026). At ~2.6 pp annually, depletion within 7–8 years absent policy correction.

5. **SOE contingent liabilities (Low probability, Medium impact).** External SOE debt of ~5% GDP not captured in government debt. Currency or operational shocks could create sovereign claims.

6. **Geopolitical/sanctions risk (Uncertain probability, Medium impact).** Deep economic ties with Russia expose Kazakhstan to secondary sanctions risk — a tail risk not quantified in Table 1.

### 4.2 Policy Recommendations

**Priority 1: Diversify fiscal revenues.** The new Tax Code is a critical opportunity: (i) raise the VAT rate and broaden the base — Kazakhstan's VAT yields ~4% of GDP vs. a 7% high-income benchmark; (ii) rationalize tax expenditures (est. 2–3% of GDP); (iii) strengthen non-oil CIT collection. A non-oil revenue target of 18–20% of GDP would materially reduce oil dependence and stabilize the NFRK.

**Priority 2: Rebuild NBK reserves and strengthen monetary credibility.** Measures: (i) strict adherence to the NFRK fiscal rule to limit pro-cyclical FX sales; (ii) resumed FX purchases during tenge strength; (iii) continued strengthening of the inflation-targeting framework — including central bank independence and communication — to durably anchor inflation at 5%.

**Priority 3: Catalyze non-oil FDI.** The halving of net FDI requires: (i) accelerated SOE privatization to attract strategic investors; (ii) improved contract enforcement and investor-state dispute resolution; (iii) NFRK co-investment in productivity-enhancing infrastructure (transport, digital, energy transition) that crowds in private capital. Raising non-oil trend growth from ~4% to 5–6% demands sustained investment in human capital and total factor productivity.

## Annex: Data Notes and Limitations

- **Source:** All figures from Table 1, World Bank *Kazakhstan Economic Update — Winter 2024–2025*. 2024–2026 = staff projections.
- **Fiscal definition:** Consolidated budget = central + local governments + NFRK. Government debt = state/local government debt + guarantees; SOE debt (~5% GDP) excluded.
- **Key gaps:** (i) NBK reserves 2025–2026 unavailable; (ii) no external debt maturity breakdown → Guidotti ratio incomputable; (iii) primary balance/interest not separately identified — debt service includes both principal and interest; (iv) no EMBI spreads or sovereign ratings in source; (v) nominal GDP in LCU not provided — ratios use implicit GDP denominators.
- **Assumptions:** Nominal GDP growth proxy = real GDP growth + CPI (eop), which may overstate nominal growth when inflation is declining. Shock scenarios assume unitary elasticities and no policy response — illustrative, not forecasts.
